# AI Daily 2026-05-06

## 1. Anthropic Launches 10 Finance Agent Templates and Microsoft 365 Claude Add-ins.

Anthropic released a suite of 10 production-ready agent templates for financial services and insurance, spanning research, client coverage, and operations workflows. Available templates include pitch builder, meeting preparer, earnings reviewer, model builder, market researcher, valuation reviewer, general ledger reconciler, month-end closer, statement auditor, and KYC screener. The agents deploy as plugins in Claude Cowork and Claude Code, and as cookbooks for Claude Managed Agents. Claude Opus 4.7 currently leads the Vals AI Finance Agent benchmark at 64.37%.

Separately, Claude now operates inside Microsoft Excel, PowerPoint, Word, and Outlook via add-ins. In Excel, it builds financial models from filings; in PowerPoint, it drafts decks that update when underlying numbers change; in Outlook it triages inbox, arranges meetings, and drafts responses. New connectors bring in Dun & Bradstreet, Financial Modeling Prep, Guidepoint, IBISWorld, SS&C IntraLinks, Third Bridge, and Verisk data. Notably, Moody's has launched an MCP app bringing proprietary credit ratings on over 600 million companies directly into the Claude workflow.

## 2. Google Releases Gemma 4 with Multi-Token Prediction Speculative Drafters, Delivering Up to 3x Inference Speedup.

Google shipped MTP speculative drafters for the Gemma 4 model family on May 5, 2026. The approach pairs a heavyweight target model — Gemma 4 31B Dense or 26B MoE — with a lightweight MTP drafter that predicts multiple future tokens in parallel before the target model verifies them in a single forward pass. If the target model accepts the draft, the entire sequence advances in one step plus an additional bonus token. The result is up to 3x improvement in tokens-per-second with zero quality degradation on benchmarks.

The speedup enables the 26B MoE and 31B Dense models to run on consumer GPUs at throughput previously achievable only on datacenter hardware. On edge devices (E2B, E4B), faster token generation preserves battery life while maintaining accuracy. The drafters have been tested against LiteRT-LM, MLX, Hugging Face Transformers, and vLLM. This is the first production MTP implementation from Google for open Gemma models and represents a concrete inference efficiency advance accessible to local developers.

## 3. SAP to Acquire Prior Labs for €1.16 Billion, Betting on Tabular Foundation Models for Enterprise Data.

SAP announced the acquisition of Prior Labs, an 18-month-old German startup, for approximately €1.16 billion. Prior Labs develops Tabular Foundation Models (TFMs) — AI systems specifically designed for structured enterprise data in databases and tables — a domain where general-purpose language models have historically underperformed. The company's TabPFN model series has been downloaded over 3 million times, indicating developer traction prior to the acquisition.

The deal signals a broader pivot in enterprise AI away from treating all data as text, toward purpose-built models for the tabular data that constitutes the backbone of enterprise operations. Also notably, the announcement explicitly stated that SAP is blocking unauthorized AI agents from its APIs, specifically naming OpenClaw as prohibited. Only SAP-endorsed architectures — Joule Agents and Nvidia's NemoClaw — are authorized. This is the first explicit enterprise-level blockade of a popular open AI agent framework and signals the emergence of a fragmented agent ecosystem where major software vendors pick winners.

## 4. Meta Faces Copyright Lawsuit Over Llama Training; Zuckerberg Personally Authorized Pirated Data Use.

Five publishers — Hachette, Macmillan, McGraw Hill, Elsevier, and Cengage — along with author Scott Turow, sued Meta and Mark Zuckerberg in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York on May 5, 2026. The lawsuit alleges Meta illegally copied millions of copyrighted works to train its Llama AI model. Internal documents cited in the filing show that after Llama 1's release in early 2023, Meta briefly considered licensing deals with a budget of up to $200 million. However, Zuckerberg personally decided to stop those efforts so Meta could "lean into the fair use strategy." A subsequent internal memo described LibGen — the source of the training data — as "a dataset we know to be pirated" and noted employees would "not disclose use of Libgen datasets used to train."

The lawsuit seeks unspecified monetary damages and alleges Meta torrented over 267 terabytes of pirated material equivalent to hundreds of millions of publications, stripped copyright management information to conceal the sources, and that Zuckerberg "personally authorized and actively encouraged the infringement." This is the first major AI training lawsuit to include CEO-level personal authorization allegations backed by internal company documents. Meta said it would "fight this lawsuit aggressively," noting prior courts have found AI training on copyrighted material can qualify as fair use.

## 5. AI R&D Automation Closer Than Thought; 60% Chance of No-Human AI Research by 2028.

Jack Clark, author of the Import AI newsletter, published a comprehensive analysis this week arguing that publicly available evidence points to roughly a 60% probability of no-human-involved automated AI R&D — systems capable of autonomously building their own successors — by the end of 2028. The conclusion is supported by multiple converging data points: SWE-Bench, the software engineering benchmark, is effectively saturated at 93.9% (Claude Mythos Preview) after launching at 2% for Claude 2 in late 2023. CORE-Bench for computational reproducibility reached 95.5% in December 2025, also saturated. MLE-Bench for machine learning engineering is at 64.4% as of February 2026, up from 16.9% at its October 2024 launch.

Most striking is the trajectory of AI systems optimizing their own training: Claude Opus 4 achieved a 2.9x mean speedup on LLM training optimization in May 2025; this rose to 16.5x with Opus 4.5 in November 2025, 30x with Opus 4.6 in February 2026, and 52x with Claude Mythos Preview in April 2026. By comparison, a human researcher typically requires 4–8 hours to achieve a 4x speedup on the same task. The METR time-horizon metric — measuring how long a task an AI can reliably complete — has grown from 30 seconds (GPT-3.5 in 2022) to approximately 12 hours (Opus 4.6 in 2026). Researchers consider a 100-hour horizon plausible by end of 2026. The implications include the risk that alignment techniques effective today may break under recursive self-improvement, and the formation of a capital-heavy, human-light economy.

## 6. Computer-Use Agents Cost 45x More Than Structured APIs for Identical Tasks, New Benchmark Shows.

Reflex.dev published a controlled benchmark comparing two Claude Sonnet agents completing the same administrative task: finding a named customer's largest order, accepting their pending reviews, and marking the order delivered. The vision agent drove the UI via screenshots and clicks; the API agent called the application's HTTP endpoints directly. The vision agent consumed 550,976 input tokens over approximately 17 minutes, completing in 53 steps. The API agent finished in 8 calls, 12,151 input tokens, and roughly 20 seconds.

The structural reason for the gap is fundamental: an agent that must see in order to act pays for seeing on every step regardless of model improvement. Better vision models reduce error rates per screenshot but do not reduce screenshot count, since each rendered interface consumes thousands of input tokens. The benchmark was facilitated by Reflex 0.9's auto-generation of HTTP endpoints from event handlers — making the structured API path nearly free for developers building internal tools. The finding has significant implications for agent architecture decisions: computer use excels when the interface is unknowable or cannot be API-fied, but for owned systems, structured APIs dominate on cost and reliability.

## 7. Meta Expands AI Underage Detection to 27 EU Countries and Brazil via Visual Age Analysis.

Meta announced the expansion of its AI-powered underage account detection system to Instagram in 27 EU countries and Brazil, and to Facebook in the United States, with UK and EU expansion for Facebook planned for June 2026. The system uses visual analysis of photos and videos — explicitly not facial recognition — to detect general age-related visual cues such as height, body proportions, and bone structure, combined with text and interaction signals. When the AI detects a likely underage user who has misrepresented their age, a supplementary AI review is applied to user reports before action is taken.

Teen Accounts, Meta's framework of AI-enforced protections for users under 18, now covers hundreds of millions of accounts globally. The approach sidesteps facial recognition by focusing on behavioral and morphological patterns rather than identity verification, a distinction Meta emphasized in response to privacy concerns. The technology was described as analyzing "visual clues about a person's age that text might miss" without identifying the specific individual. The rollout represents the largest automated underage enforcement system deployed by a major social platform to date.

## 8. GPT-5.5 Instant Becomes Default ChatGPT Model; Memory Sourcing Feature Expands to All Consumer Tiers.

OpenAI's GPT-5.5 Instant replaced GPT-5.3 Instant as the default ChatGPT model for all users on May 6, 2026. GPT-5.3 will remain accessible to paid users for three months via model settings before being retired. The new model scores 81.2 on AIME 2025 mathematics (up from 65.4 for GPT-5.3) and 76 on MMMU-Pro multimodal benchmarks (up from 69.2). OpenAI reports reduced hallucination rates in law, medicine, and finance — domains where accuracy is critical.

A parallel launch this week is Memory Sourcing, which shows users which conversations and files ChatGPT used to generate responses in a given exchange. Users can delete or correct specific sources. The feature was previously limited to Pro subscribers and is now expanding to all consumer plans across web, with mobile rollout forthcoming. The combination of a new default model, an expanding memory layer, and a three-month deprecation window for the predecessor reflects OpenAI's accelerating model replacement cadence.

Source: TechCrunch / OpenAI | 2026-05-05
