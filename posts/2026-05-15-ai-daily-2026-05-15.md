# AI Daily 2026-05-15

## 1. Cerebras surges 108% in 2026's opening tech IPO, reaching a $66 billion valuation.

Cerebras Systems raised $5.5 billion in its IPO on Thursday, pricing 30 million shares at $185 — above its raised range of $150 to $160 — after originally filing at $115 to $125. Shares opened at $385, up 108%, and closed the day at $311, translating to a $66 billion valuation. At the IPO price, the fully diluted valuation was $56.4 billion. Co-founder and CEO Andrew Feldman's stake was worth nearly $1.9 billion, and co-founder and CTO Sean Lie's stake approximately $1 billion. A year ago, the IPO looked impossible: a 2024 filing was shelved after CFIUS scrutiny over Abu Dhabi-based Group 42, which at the time accounted for nearly all Cerebras revenue. The company returned to market in April 2026 after reporting $510 million in 2025 revenue — up 76% year over year — and a swing to $237.8 million in net income, compared with nearly $500 million in losses the prior year. Named customers include OpenAI, G42, Mohamed bin Zayed University of Artificial Intelligence (MBZUAI), and Amazon Web Services. The stock rose again in after-hours trading following the close.

## 2. Recursive Superintelligence exits stealth with $650 million and a team including Peter Norvig and Tim Rocktäschel.

Richard Socher's Recursive Superintelligence launched on Wednesday with $650 million in funding, aiming to build recursively self-improving AI systems that autonomously identify their own weaknesses and redesign themselves to fix them. The team includes Peter Norvig, AI pioneer and co-author of the standard AI textbook; Tim Shi, co-founder of Cresta (a unicorn); and Tim Rocktäschel, who led open-endedness and self-improvement research at Google DeepMind, including work on rainbow teaming — a co-evolution technique where one AI attacks a target model and another defends, iterating through millions of adversarial cycles. Socher's stated approach is open-endedness: a process similar to biological evolution where systems co-adapt indefinitely, generating continuous novelty rather than converging to a fixed capability ceiling. The company plans to automate the full cycle of AI research ideation, implementation, and validation. Socher said the team expects to ship products "in quarters, not years." The company departs from pure research labs by targeting productization from the start.

## 3. OpenAI brings Codex mobile controls to ChatGPT, giving 4 million weekly users remote agent supervision.

OpenAI's coding agent Codex — now used by more than 4 million people per week — is rolling out in preview inside the ChatGPT mobile app on iOS and Android across all subscription plans. Mobile users can review agent outputs, approve or reject individual commands, switch models mid-session, inspect code diffs, and start new agent threads. Remote SSH connections and Hooks (automated trigger-action workflows) are generally available on all plans. Scoped programmatic access tokens target Enterprise and Business customers for CI and automation use cases. HIPAA-compliant local Codex execution is available for eligible ChatGPT Enterprise workspaces. The launch signals a shift from browser-based agent sessions toward persistent, device-distributed agent supervision, as developers increasingly need to manage long-running coding tasks from mobile contexts.

## 4. BenchJack audit finds near-universal reward-hacking vulnerabilities across 10 major agent benchmarks.

Researchers at UC Berkeley published BenchJack, an automated adversarial auditing framework that evaluated 10 popular agent benchmarks covering software engineering, web, desktop, and terminal tasks. The study synthesized exploits that achieved near-perfect scores on most benchmarks without actually completing the target tasks. Across the 10 benchmarks, 219 distinct flaws were identified and grouped into eight recurring vulnerability classes. An iterative repair pipeline reduced the proportion of hackable tasks from near 100% to under 10% on four of the benchmarks. The findings indicate that current agent evaluation infrastructure is highly susceptible to reward-hacking — agents optimizing for benchmark metrics rather than genuine task completion — raising questions about the reliability of reported benchmark performance as a proxy for real-world agent capability. The authors propose the repair pipeline as a model for ongoing benchmark hardening.

## 5. ATLAS introduces functional tokens that bridge agentic visual operations and latent reasoning in a single vocabulary.

ATLAS, a framework from researchers at The Chinese University of Hong Kong and others, proposes that a single ordinary tokenizer word — termed a functional token — can serve simultaneously as an agentic visual operation (calling an external tool) and a latent visual reasoning unit (encoding internal state). No new architecture or visual supervision is required; functional tokens are standard vocabulary items generated via next-token prediction. The approach uses a curated 178,000-example dataset (ATLAS-178K) spanning more than 40 visual reasoning tasks and a Latent-Anchored GRPO (LA-GRPO) training method to address token sparsity during RL. On the BLINK benchmark, average accuracy rose from 22.8% with Qwen2.5-VL to 51.3% with ATLAS LA-GRPO. Functional tokens represent 2.3% of generated tokens on average. The authors argue the approach avoids the context-switching latency of agentic tool-calling systems while maintaining compatibility with standard SFT and RL training pipelines.

## 6. Mistletoe attack collapses speculative decoding speedup from 5.47x to 1.83x while preserving output quality.

A team from Tsinghua University and others published Mistletoe, a stealthy attack targeting the acceptance mechanism of speculative decoding — an increasingly widespread LLM inference acceleration technique. Rather than corrupting model outputs, Mistletoe targets the drafter-target agreement rate, degrading the average accepted token length (τ) while keeping the target model's visible behavior semantically unchanged. The method uses null-space projected optimization to jointly minimize acceptance rate and semantic drift, projecting degradation gradients away from semantic-preserving directions. On MT-Bench, average speed-up collapsed from a measured 5.47x baseline to 1.83x in one ablation, with τ falling from 5.95 to 2.79. Across multiple setups, the relative reduction in speed-up reached 51.7%. The authors recommend monitoring accepted-length distributions in production speculative decoding systems and designing more robust verification mechanisms. This is the first paper to characterize a mechanism-level attack surface introduced by LLM inference acceleration, not merely content-generation robustness.

Source: arXiv (cs.CL) | 2026-05-15

## 7. Physics-R1 audit finds 134 near-duplicates and a 46-point format gradient in vision-language physics benchmarks.

A systematic three-stage audit of multimodal physics benchmarks documents widespread contamination and measurement artifacts in how the field evaluates vision-language reasoning. Using a Jaccard → embedding cosine → LLM-judge pipeline, researchers found 134 near-duplicates and 4,846 paraphrase candidates in the SciInstruct training set alone, despite zero hits on standard 5-gram Jaccard. On 59 paired Estonian-English olympiad problems, Sonnet 4.5 scored 30.5% on English originals versus 13.6% on Estonian translations — a 16.9 percentage point gap with statistical significance (McNemar p=0.021). On identical model weights, the same system scored 79.7% on multiple-choice PhyX but only 33.4% on open-ended PhysOlym-A, a 46-point format-and-novelty gradient. The researchers release PhysCorp-A (6,432 audited multimodal records), PhysR1Corp (2,268 closed-loop RL records), and PhysOlym-A (500-problem held-out olympiad evaluation). They also release Physics-R1, a reference recipe that improves the Qwen3-VL-8B base by +18.3 pp on PhysOlym-A.

Source: arXiv (cs.CL) | 2026-05-15
